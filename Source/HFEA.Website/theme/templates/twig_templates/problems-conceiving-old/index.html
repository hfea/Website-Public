When to see your doctor

If you’ve been having regular, unprotected sex (two or three times a week) for over a year and you haven’t gotten pregnant you should see your GP. You should see your GP sooner if you are aged 36 or over, have been diagnosed with a cause of infertility or have a history of problems which may affect your fertility. More on when to get medical advice.

Why might I be struggling to get pregnant?

Medical conditions

Common reasons why couples have problems getting pregnant range from gynaecological problems to low sperm count.

Female

Cancer, polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), endometriosis, chlamydia, diabetes, epilepsy and thyroid and bowel diseases; conditions affecting the uterus; gynaecological problems such as previous ectopic pregnancy or having had more than one miscarriage; other problems with ovulation (the monthly release of the egg), sexually transmitted diseases

Male

A lower sperm count, or sperm with an unusual shape or movement; diabetes; a past bacterial infection; problems with the tubes carrying sperm; difficulty getting an erection or ejaculating; having had inflamed testes (orchitis); having received medical treatment such as drug treatment, radiotherapy or surgery – for example to correct a hernia, undescended testes or twisted testicles; sexually transmitted diseases

Age

The age of the woman has a major impact on your chances of getting pregnant. Of women aged 35 and under who have regular unprotected sexual intercourse, 95% will get pregnant within three years, while by the age of 38 only 75% of women will do so.

Lifestyle factors

Lifestyle factors such as being overweight or underweight, smoking, drinking, stress or having a job that involves contact with chemicals or radiation can all lessen your chances of getting pregnant. Visit NHS Choices for more information on preparing your body for pregnancy.

Genetics

In some cases fertility problems are genetic – talk to your doctor about whether this might apply to you.
